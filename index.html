I am an emeritus professor in the Statistics department at the University of Warwick. My research interests lie mostly in probability theory: random processes, stochastic geometry, stochastic calculus, computer algebra in statistics and probability, and perfect simulation.

My occasional blog Random Curiosities records some occasional thoughts arising when teaching or researching.

Contact me at w dot s dot kendall at warwick dot ac dot uk.

Recent and partial biography

I served as President of the Bernoulli Society (2013-2015), and also served on the Council of the International Statistical Institute (2015-2019). I was one of two founding co-directors of APTS (serving from October 2007 to September 2016), first with David Firth, then with Adam Johansen. A few years ago I found myself back on the APTS Executive Committee for a brief period: deja vu all over again.

Recent research projects

  • Anglo-Saxon Placenames and Point Processes.

Github software repositories

These repositories hold software related to miscellaneous computing projects:

  1. Detector Checker, funded by The Alan Turing Institute.
    This corresponds to two repositories DetectorChecker code and DetectorChecker WebApp. It concerns R software developed in collaboration with my colleague Julia Brettschneider, and Turing Institute research software engineers Tomas Lazauskas and Oscar Giles.

This project developed R software and a Web App to aid in the assessment of damage to CT scanners arising from exposure to high energy radiation. While the target application concerns CT scanners, this package can also be used to analyze screen damage arising from other sources.
Initially the Web App was available online, but host funding expired. The Web App repository includes instructions for running the Web App in a docker image. The Detector Checker project is no longer being developed, though the repositories remain accessible.

  1. LaTeX-writing:
    This project has developed LaTeX packages to assist in writing mathematics (namely, WSKmaths.cls) and annotating it with revision notes et cetera (namely WSKtracking.cls). The packages are still under occasional development, and I use them all the time!
